4Detailed Session Notes
Captured live during each lecture. The chronological build-up of the technical curriculum.
Day 1 · Morning · Dr. Kumar Muthuraman

Neurons, Networks & Training

Artificial neurons as weighted-sum + bias units, stacked into layers, trained by nudging millions of randomly-initialized weights via gradients.

The neuron

  • X₁..Xₙ × W₁..Wₙ + bias B → activation function → output (e.g., yes/no)
  • Bias = activation threshold; can be viewed as W₀ × constant input
  • Activation can be jumpy or smooth — the building block is simple, the power comes from combination

Training mechanics

  • Initialize weights randomly → predict → compare to ground truth → derivatives tell you how to nudge each "dial"
  • Repeat millions of times; compute scale is what makes it feasible
  • Toy example: predict cups sold from price, promotion, school-in-session — 35 parameters, prediction starts at −37 vs actual ~100
  • If convergence is poor: more data, or more model complexity, then retry

Scientific process

  • Transformers followed thousands of failed experiments — papers publish the winner + post-hoc rationale
  • This creates an illusion of clairvoyance about architecture; real practice is iteration
Day 1 · Morning (cont.)

Neural Network Fundamentals & Q&A

Capacity-vs-specificity trade-offs, training mechanics, RNN vs Transformer memory, hallucination as pattern completion.

Capacity vs domain specificity

  • 80/20 principle: flexible enough to learn the task, not so flexible it needs impractical data/compute
  • Domain-specific corpus → domain-specific behavior; mixing domains can hurt
  • Often better to train multiple specialized models than one oversized general one

Training loop reality

  • Predict next character/token → measure error → derivatives → tiny weight updates → repeat across millions of tokens & many epochs
  • After deployment, weights are fixed; prompting does not retrain the model

Memory & architecture

  • Don't equate networks to brains — biological brains rewire dynamically; networks have fixed architecture during inference
  • Sequence tasks need a memory mechanism; Transformers + attention beat older RNNs at language

Hallucination & knowledge graphs

  • Hallucination = predicting a plausible continuation, not "lying"
  • Knowledge graphs are promising but current integrations are ad hoc and inconclusive

Demo

  • Character-level RNN trained on 2M chars of real-estate text — domain emerged across epochs
  • Instructor will share Jupyter notebook, dataset, and slides
Day 1 · Afternoon · Dr. Zena Drakou

Word Embeddings, LLMs & Model Bias

From static word vectors (word2vec) to contextual Transformer embeddings, with hard truths about bias and hallucination.

Embeddings

  • Numerical vectors are required to feed language into LLMs
  • Dimensionality (e.g., 300 vs 400) is a tunable design choice
  • Different corpora produce different embeddings for the same word — finance vs conservation domains

Word2Vec mechanics

  • 10k vocab, one-hot input → hidden layer (= embedding size) → softmax over vocab to predict context
  • Loss + backprop iterate weights until correct context words get high probability
  • Hidden-layer activations after training are the embeddings
  • Context window (±3 words typical) defines which pairs train

Semantic geometry

  • Cosine similarity measures closeness
  • Vector arithmetic captures relations: king − man + woman ≈ queen; plurality and gender vectors emerge naturally

Bias & hallucination

  • Models inherit biases of training data — early face-recognition trained mostly on white people
  • Hallucinations risk being amplified if used as training signal in next iteration
  • Mitigations: filtering, safety checks, vetted domain-specific fine-tuning corpora

Transformers preview

  • Unlike static word2vec, Transformers produce contextual embeddings — same word, different vector depending on sentence
Day 1 · Late Afternoon

Intro to Transformers & Attention

From seq2seq RNN bottlenecks → attention → BERT vs GPT. Demo: swap word vectors for transformer embeddings → measurable accuracy lift on movie-review classification.

NLP timeline

  • Static word vectors → seq2seq encoder/decoder RNNs (early 2000s–2013) → attention (2017) → BERT, GPT
  • RNN translation compressed entire input into a fixed-size vector → information loss for long inputs

Attention conceptually

  • Computes weighted relationships between every pair of tokens → context-aware embedding per token
  • Function words get low weight; semantically heavy words get high weight
  • Example: "blue" as color vs emotion resolved by attention

Encoder–decoder + attention

  • Decoder can look back at encoder states selectively — no more single bottleneck vector

Practical demo

  • Notebook: same classifier, only embeddings swapped (word vectors → transformer) → accuracy improves on movie-review sentiment

BERT vs GPT

  • BERT: bidirectional → encoding/classification
  • GPT: autoregressive → generation
Day 2 · Morning · Dr. Dan Mitchell

Transformer Internals: Tokenization, Positional Encoding, Attention

The deep technical session — queries/keys/values via the library analogy, with a numerical "Dan is here" self-attention walkthrough.

Tokenization & embeddings

  • LLaMA uses subword tokenization for efficient vocab math; word-level is more intuitive for teaching
  • Embeddings are looked up from a dictionary, then modified by the Transformer layers
  • Token vectors themselves can be updated during training (gradient descent)

Positional encoding

  • Without position info, "Dan saw Pat" and "Pat saw Dan" produce identical math
  • Original Attention paper used sinusoidal (trig-based) encodings — hand-crafted to approximate orthogonality across many positions
  • Modern alternatives: learned positional vectors and RoPE (rotary positional encoding)
  • Constraint: you can't have more orthogonal vectors than embedding dimensionality

Attention = library analogy

  • Query = your report topic
  • Keys = book titles you skim to choose which books are relevant
  • Values = book contents you actually read & combine
  • Math: dot(Q,K) → softmax → weighted average of V

Self-attention walkthrough

  • "Dan is here" with 3-element vectors per token
  • Use each token in turn as the query; all tokens as keys; weighted-average their values
  • Result: each token gets an updated, context-aware vector

Scale reality

  • Training a frontier LLM = months on thousands of GPUs — generally infeasible solo
  • Use pre-trained models; fine-tune embeddings + positional vectors as needed
Day 2 · Mid-morning

Transformers & LLMs Workshop

How LLMs generate text, training objective, MoE / state-space architectures, and the legal / privacy minefield.

Generation loop

  • Decoder: token vectors → positional → multi-head attention → feed-forward → probability over ~40k vocab
  • Sample one token (temperature controls randomness) → append to context → repeat until EOS

Training objective

  • Maximum-likelihood next-token prediction across massive corpora
  • Probability assigned to the true next token = quality proxy → gradient descent

Data & legal exposure

  • Web scraping has triggered lawsuits (news publishers); private repos have been accessed
  • Garbage-in / garbage-out — training data quality directly affects reliability

Architectural advances

  • Mixture-of-Experts (MoE): route tokens to a subset of expert networks → more params, less compute per inference
  • State-space models: evolving state vector avoids reprocessing entire history → cheaper long-context

Fine-tuning & agents

  • Fine-tuning improves style but doesn't guarantee facts — can amplify hallucinations
  • Agents (LLM + tools/SQL/code) yield more reliable, fact-backed answers
  • For structured data: have the LLM generate the query, execute it externally, summarize results

Privacy guidance

  • Web interface inputs may be added to training corpora — prefer API or enterprise plan for sensitive content
Day 2 · Afternoon

Agent Workflows & Intro to RAG

Why structured agent workflows beat free-form agents in business, and how RAG grounds an LLM in real source material.

Workflows vs free-form agents

  • Agent workflow: human-designed flowchart, LLM picks which branch
  • Pure agents are flexible but unpredictable — risky for production
  • Example: IT ticket router uses LLM to classify, check FAQ, draft response

RAG pipeline

  • Query → semantic search → retrieve top chunks → augment prompt → generate
  • Grounded answers reduce hallucination dramatically vs LLM-alone

Embeddings & retrieval

  • Encode query and chunks → dot-product similarity → rank
  • Chunk vector = average of token vectors, OR use the BOS/[CLS] token — quality varies
  • Off-the-shelf BERT isn't optimized for retrieval without fine-tuning

Fine-tuning embeddings

  • Contrastive training with similar/dissimilar pairs
  • Sources: StackOverflow duplicate Qs, Reddit ELI5 question/answer threads

Tools

  • sentence-transformers Python library
  • Hugging Face leaderboard for picking embedding models
Day 2 · Late Afternoon · Hands-On

RAG & Advanced Retrieval Workshop

Built a RAG system in a few lines. Then layered re-ranking, generate-and-embed, jargon enrichment, and an LLM router across multiple databases.

Pragmatic stack

  • Minimal RAG = a handful of lines: chunk → embed → query → top-k → prompt → generate
  • LangChain / generic-agent frameworks: useful for prototyping, fragile for production — self-implement core pieces when shipping

Re-ranking strategies

  • Cross-encoder pairwise: high accuracy, expensive — only run on a small candidate set
  • Inverse-rank aggregation: run multiple embedding models, sum weighted inverse ranks → cheap consensus
  • LLM re-ranker: ask the model to order a fixed candidate list
  • Pattern: dense retriever shortlists → re-ranker refines → keeps latency manageable

Beyond plain similarity

  • Generate-and-embed: have the LLM draft a candidate answer, embed that, retrieve chunks similar to it (answer-to-answer matching often beats question-to-document)
  • Jargon enrichment (recent FB paper): use an LLM to extract domain keywords/synonyms per chunk → fast BM25-style keyword lookups for technical queries

Multi-database orchestration

  • LLM as router: decides which database (sales / specs / customer comms) and which retrieval method to call
  • Can extend search depth, fall back, or honestly return "I don't know" when results are weak

Use cases observed

  • Customer support bots, internal Slack bots, repair-manual Q&A
  • Hands-on lab: RAG over the 10-K filing of a public company
Day 2 · Evening · Dr. Gaylen Paulson

Negotiation Workshop — Foundations

Trust = accepting vulnerability. Learn-mode posture, real stories of bad anchoring, and a paired sugar-bowl simulation.

Framing

  • The session is for learning, not performing — bring vulnerability, not skill
  • Goal: deepen technical depth while broadening to T-shaped awareness

Trust

  • Trust = accepting vulnerability. Asking for trust = asking someone to expose themselves to risk
  • Framing & communication choices determine whether others will accept that vulnerability

Stories that stuck

  • Princeton salary anchor: low-anchoring a faculty hire created retention & poaching exposure
  • Sisters & the orange: 50/50 split looked fair but was suboptimal — one wanted juice, one wanted peel. Find interests, not positions

Preparation as the lever

  • Many conflicts are recurring & predictable — anticipate & design ahead
  • Wording shapes behavior: "can we explore together?" beats "let's negotiate"

Sugar-bowl simulation

  • 5–7 min prep, paired with a new partner, limited info
  • Embellish OK; gross changes not OK; lying realistic
  • If deal: record + sign + keep confidential until debrief. If no deal: record last offers same way
Day 2 · Evening (cont.)

Negotiation Strategies: Anchors, BATNA, Connection

The tactical playbook: anchor optimistically, prepare four things, visit your third choice first, and ask far more follow-up questions than you think.

Anchoring & opening offers

  • First offers anchor the entire range — go first when you understand the counterpart's bottom
  • Aspirations should be ambitious and defensible — always be ready to explain the number

The negotiator's dilemma

  • Sharing info enables joint gains and invites exploitation
  • Decide disclosure strategy in prep, anchored to your goals + BATNA

The four-part prep framework

  • Aspirations — what you hope to achieve
  • Reservation price — minimum acceptable
  • BATNA — Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement
  • Interests — the underlying why

Two rules for alternatives

  • Cultivate at least 3 options — never depend on one supplier or buyer
  • Visit your 3rd choice first. Concretizes alternatives, gives you real terms, strengthens leverage with the preferred party

Resisting pressure tactics

  • Manufactured urgency (countdowns, "limited quantity") = sales tactic, not reality
  • Counter: ask clarifying questions ("what about past customers?", "any rule exceptions?") instead of reacting

Building connection

  • Prepare topics before the meeting — small effort, big return
  • Experts ask questions 22–24% of the time
  • Follow-up questions (which require listening) are the highest-leverage move for rapport
  • Avoid loaded words ("negotiator", "negotiate") — collaborative phrasing lowers defenses

5What I Can Now Apply
The handful of moves that actually transfer to work this week.
01

Build a minimal RAG before reaching for a framework

Chunk → embed → top-k → augment → generate. Few lines of code. Skip LangChain abstractions until you understand the pieces — frameworks are fragile in production.

02

Layer re-ranking only where it pays

Use a cheap dense retriever to shortlist; apply cross-encoder pairwise scoring or LLM re-ranking on the small candidate set. Inverse-rank aggregation is essentially free when combining multiple embedding models.

03

Try generate-and-embed when queries don't match document phrasing

Have the LLM draft a candidate answer, embed that, then retrieve. Answer-to-answer matching often beats question-to-document.

04

Have an LLM route across specialized stores

Don't merge everything into one index. Let the LLM pick which database (sales, specs, comms) and which retrieval method to call — and to honestly say "I don't know" when results are weak.

05

For structured data, generate the query — don't analyze in the LLM

Have the model write SQL/code; execute it externally; summarize the result. This dodges the worst hallucination class.

06

Pick the privacy mode deliberately

Web UI → inputs may be added to training corpora. For anything sensitive, default to API or enterprise plan.

07

Prep every negotiation in four lines

Aspiration · Reservation · BATNA · Interests. Cultivate ≥3 alternatives. Visit your third choice first.

08

Ask more follow-up questions

Experts ask 22–24% of the time; novices much less. Follow-ups are the cheapest, highest-signal way to build rapport and surface real interests.

09

Don't equate networks with brains, or hallucination with lying

Networks have fixed inference architecture. Hallucination = probabilistic pattern completion. Both reframings change how you scope risk and explain limits to non-technical stakeholders.

10

Trust the iteration over the published narrative

Transformers were the survivor of thousands of failed experiments. Don't be intimidated by post-hoc clean stories — run the experiment.
